Choking

Differential Diagnosis

  • Anaphylaxis
  • Angioedema
  • Asthma
  • Cerebrovascular accident
  • Croup
  • Epiglottitis
  • Foreign body aspiration
  • Upper respiratory infection

Basic Medical Care

  1. Medical Initial Assessment Protocol or Pediatric Initial Assessment Protocol
  2. Maintain airway; suction as needed
  3. Assess vital signs
  4. Provide supplemenmtal oxygen per patient condition to maintain SpO2 = 94 - 97%
  5. Attempt to remove source of exposure (e.g. stinger) as possible
  6. Partial or complete airway obstruction due to foreign body
    1. Patient conscious
      1. Encourage coughing
      2. Coughing ineffective → deliver 5 sub diaphragmatic abdominal thrusts
      3. Sub diaphragmatic thrusts ineffective (or pregnant patient) → deliver chest thrusts
      4. Continue thrusts until obstruction is relieved or unconsciousness occurs
    2. Infant < 1 year of age conscious
      1. If child coughing or making sounds, observe for further obstruction
      2. If choking progresses and cough is ineffective → deliver 5 back blows then 5 less forceful chest thrusts
      3. Continue cycle of back blows and chest thrusts until obstruction relieved
    3. Patient unconscious
      1. Open airway using head tilt-chin lift and check oral cavity for foreign body
      2. Use finger sweep as indicated if material is visualized
        • Do not perform blind finger sweeps
      3. Attempt ventilations
      4. Reposition airway as indicated to optimized ventilations
      5. Repeat above until obstruction relieved or intubation equipment prepped
      6. Initiate CPR as indicated by patient condition

Advanced Medical Care

  1. For unconscious patients with airway obstruction unrelieved by methods outlined above, use laryngoscope to visualize posterior pharynx and larynx
    1. Remove any observed foreign material with suction or Magill forceps
  2. Advanced Airway management as indicated
    1. Airway: Intubation Protocol as indicated
    2. Airway: BIAD Protocol as indicated
  3. Ensure proper tube placement using capnometry, SpO2 and ventilate with 100% oxygen
  4. Obtain rhythm strip and refer to appropriate protocol as indicated

Additional Considerations

  • Many choking episodes will be resolved prior to EMS arrival on the scene
    • A thorough assessment should be performed on all patients regardless of symptoms
  • Blind finger sweeps without direct visualization of foreign material are contraindicated in all patients
  • Any infant choking episode associated with a period of apnea or cyanosis should be transported regardless of appearance on arrival
  • Choking may be an early sign for stroke onset
  • Aspiration is often associated with a choking episode
  • In situations where a complete obstruction is below the level of the vocal cords, the only option may be to perform intubation via standard technique and advance the endotracheal tube into a mainstem bronchus in effort to advance the foreign body into that bronchus
    • Then withdrawal the endotracheal tube so the distal end is at typical depth within the trachea and ventilate as usual
    • Provider will only be ventilating one lung in this instance, but effective oxygenation and ventilation can still occur utilizing a single lung
